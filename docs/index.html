What is Urban Access?

A routing tool that reflects real sidewalk conditions — not just distance and traffic — so that people with mobility impairments can plan journeys that are actually possible for them.

"What if your navigation app actually understood what it's like to use a wheelchair?"

Existing tools like Google Maps treat all pedestrian routes as equivalent. They don't know whether a curb cut is present, whether a ramp is graded for a power wheelchair, or whether a construction zone has blocked an accessible path entirely.

Urban Access changes that. Using AI-scored street-level images from Mapillary, a fine-tuned Vision Transformer, and a large language model that synthesises accessibility signals into plain-language directions, Urban Access recommends the best route for your specific mobility aid — with explanations of why.

The project supports six mobility aid types: manual wheelchair, electric wheelchair, walker, walking cane, mobility scooter, and mobility impairment without aid.

The Problem

Montreal is home to over 200,000 residents with mobility impairments. The city has invested in accessible infrastructure — but the gap is in the routes between destinations, not the destinations themselves.

🚧

Sidewalk Barriers

Cracked or uneven pavement, missing curb cuts, cobblestone surfaces, and construction zones create unpredictable hazards that change daily.

🏔️

Terrain & Grade

Steep inclines and elevation changes are passable for a walking cane but can be impassable for a manual wheelchair or mobility scooter.

🌨️

Seasonal Changes

Montreal winters dramatically change sidewalk conditions. Snow and ice accumulation can make an otherwise accessible route unsafe overnight.

🗺️

The Last-Mile Gap

A government building may be fully accessible — but if the 400 metres of sidewalk leading to it are not, it is effectively inaccessible. Existing tools miss this entirely.

Three AI Layers, One Recommendation

Urban Access combines computer vision, vision-language models, and a large language model into a single seamless routing pipeline.

1. Vision Transformer (ViT)

A fine-tuned ViT scores each street-level image for accessibility across six mobility aid types. Trained on 31,887 labelled Montreal images. Best mean F1: 0.726.

2. VLM Secondary Scoring

Gemini 2.5 Flash provides richer contextual assessments at the most uncertain or critical route points — intersections, ramps, construction zones — with natural-language justifications.

3. LLM Route Synthesis

An open-source LLM synthesises ViT scores, VLM annotations, pedestrian safety data, construction data, gradient, and user feedback into a ranked route recommendation with plain-language explanations.

Grounded in Community

The system's design was shaped from the start by people with direct experience of accessibility barriers — not assumed by the team.

Urban Access consulted with disability advocates and urban planning researchers before writing a line of model code. Our survey campaign reached 30+ disability-focused community groups across Montreal to build ground-truth accessibility labels.

Two key advisors shaped the project's direction:

  • Rashid Mushkani — urban planning researcher who introduced the concept of the "last-mile gap" in accessibility, directly shaping our routing focus
  • Laurence Parent — Plateau-Mont-Royal city councillor who provided iterative feedback throughout development to validate that outputs reflect real disabled users' needs
